Improved service can mitigate several Town problems. Public transit improvements serve unlicensed youth and seniors, boosting independence and mental health by enabling them to reach academic activities or social events without a ride. A lack of quality transit damages extracurricular programs and creates a less well-connected community. Quality public transit can also address the Town's traffic concerns. Already, Route 27 sees 1,278 boardings a day ([VTA Open Data](https://data.vta.org/pages/ridership-by-stop)), meaning hundreds of cars off the roads. An improved route, focused on schools, will take cars out of dangerous, gridlocked drop-off situations. Meanwhile, these driving reductions significantly cut back damaging CO2 emissions. We ran the numbers to create a revived Route 27 that can better serve vulnerable communities. Several changes will produce quantifiable benefits for Town residents, including through reduced travel time, increased accessibility, and much more. - heading: "Why focus on schools?" body: | Public transit thrives where large groups of people congregate at specific, regular, and predictable times. Downtown areas and shopping centers often see large numbers of people, but at different times of day. Workplaces are spread throughout Los Gatos, making coverage more difficult. Furthermore, with the advent of online shopping, the popularization of remote work, and the ability to get takeout delivered straight to your door, those with the means often avoid travel altogether. But schools see hundreds or even thousands of unlicensed individuals with limited independent travel options arriving and leaving in the same 10-minute timeframe, something unlikely to be substantially reduced by the Internet. Therefore, schools are often a major target for transit agencies looking for the consistent ridership off which to build a program. All the agency must do is market themselves to students and ensure timely arrivals for drop-off and pickup. VTA currently does neither of these things. About half of Union and LGHS students who are driven to school are unaware of the existence of the local bus route, and the bus route often arrives half an hour from school start and end times. Simple, low-cost or free reforms at schools can therefore create large increases in convenience for students, resulting in large long-term ridership gains.
